Fluid-structure interaction

Fluid-structure interaction (FSI) is the coupling of an external flow with deformable structures. Applications include flexible aircraft wings, blood flow in arteries, wind turbines, and flow induced vibrations of bridges.

FSI algorithms either use a single solver for the fluid and solid (monolithic) or independent solvers for each domain (partitioned).

Partitioned algorithms offer many benefits including computational efficiency and modularity. However most partitioned algorithms suffer from instabilities when added-mass effects are large. To understand added-mass, consider an experiment where we drop a hammer and a feather.

In a vaccuum, the feather and hammer will hit the ground at the same time. When immersed in a fluid, the feather and hammer will displace fluid while dropping. The hammer is much heavier compared to the displaced fluid, so its motion will be roughly the same in a vacuum. On the other hand, the feather is light compared to the displaced fluid, so it will drop slower. Added-mass effects are large for a feather, but small for a hammer.

I worked on developing the added-mass partitioned (AMP) scheme, which is a partitioned scheme that is insensitive to added-mass instabilities. This algorithm allows us to efficiently and robustly simulate FSI problems.
